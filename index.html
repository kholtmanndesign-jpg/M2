Biography

Martin Luther King Jr. (1929–1968) was a Baptist minister and one of the most influential leaders of the American civil rights movement. He advocated for equality through nonviolent protest and civil disobedience, challenging systems of segregation and discrimination in the United States.

Born in Atlanta, Georgia, King studied theology and became a pastor before emerging as a national figure during the Montgomery bus boycott in the mid‑1950s. His leadership connected religious conviction with a call for social justice and human rights.

Quick facts
  • Born: January 15, 1929, Atlanta, Georgia
  • Occupation: Baptist minister, civil rights leader
  • Major award: Nobel Peace Prize, 1964
  • Died: April 4, 1968, Memphis, Tennessee

Key events

  • 1955: Helps lead the Montgomery bus boycott after Rosa Parks is arrested, challenging segregated public transportation.
  • 1957: Becomes the first president of the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC), organizing nonviolent campaigns across the South.
  • 1963: Helps lead the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom, where he delivers the famous “I Have a Dream” speech.
  • 1964: Receives the Nobel Peace Prize for his leadership in the struggle for civil rights through nonviolent means.
  • 1965: Plays a major role in the Selma to Montgomery marches, which help lead to the Voting Rights Act of 1965.

“I Have a Dream”

On August 28, 1963, during the March on Washington, King delivered his “I Have a Dream” speech at the Lincoln Memorial. In this address, he spoke about his vision of a United States where people would be judged by their character rather than the color of their skin.

“I have a dream that one day this nation will rise up and live out the true meaning of its creed: ‘We hold these truths to be self‑evident, that all men are created equal.’”

The speech became one of the most famous in American history and continues to inspire movements for equality, justice, and human rights around the world.

Legacy

Martin Luther King Jr.’s work helped lead to major civil rights laws, including legislation that outlawed segregation and protected voting rights. His approach to nonviolence influenced activists across the globe, from anti‑apartheid movements to modern campaigns for social justice.

In the United States, a federal holiday honors his life and work each January. Schools, communities, and organizations often mark the day with service projects and discussions focused on equality, justice, and community building.
